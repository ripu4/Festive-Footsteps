Diwali: The Festival of Lights

Diwali, also known as the Festival of Lights, is one of the most celebrated festivals in India. It's a time for family gatherings, lighting oil lamps, exchanging sweets, and praying for prosperity. Here's a step-by-step guide on how to celebrate Diwali, including the rituals, best practices, and ways to make the most of this joyous occasion.



Pre-Diwali Preparations

Diwali celebrations typically start days before the main festival. Preparing your home and heart for the festivities is a key part of the tradition.

Cleaning is a symbolic way to invite Goddess Lakshmi, the deity of wealth, into your home. Dust and declutter every corner, and many people even repaint or redecorate their homes.

Purchase new clothes, sweets, gifts, and decorative items like lanterns and diyas (oil lamps). It's considered auspicious to buy gold, silver, or new household items during this period.


Rituals On Diwali Day

On Diwali morning, people take a special bath called "Abhyanga Snan" using oil and traditional herbs. This is believed to purify the body and soul, and mark the start of a new year.

After the oil bath, dress in new traditional clothes. Bright-colored outfits, especially in red, yellow, or gold, are preferred as they are considered auspicious.

Make vibrant rangoli designs on your doorstep or in the courtyard using colored powders, flowers, and rice. Rangoli is believed to bring prosperity and positivity to the household.

As the sun sets, light diyas (small clay oil lamps) and place them around the house, especially near windows, doors, and balconies. The idea is to illuminate your home and drive away darkness, symbolizing the triumph of light over evil.


Puja (Worship)

The Diwali puja, especially Lakshmi Puja, is one of the most important rituals. It is performed to seek blessings from Goddess Lakshmi and Lord Ganesha.

Arrange idols or images of Goddess Lakshmi and Lord Ganesha on a clean altar. Decorate the space with flowers, and light incense sticks and diyas.

Prepare sweets, fruits, and other offerings like rice, turmeric, and flowers to present during the puja. Traditional sweets like laddoos, barfis, and kheer are commonly used.

Begin by worshipping Lord Ganesha to remove any obstacles.

Then perform Lakshmi Puja by chanting mantras, offering flowers, sweets, and lighting incense.

Many families also perform an Aarti (a ceremonial prayer with a lit lamp) to conclude the puja.


Celebrating the Night

After the puja, the evening is dedicated to celebrating with loved ones.

Prepare or order a lavish meal, including traditional dishes like puris, curries, and sweets. Share these delicacies with family and friends.

Bursting firecrackers is a longstanding tradition during Diwali. However, in recent years, there has been a shift towards eco-friendly celebrations with less noise and pollution. Consider using green firecrackers or celebrating with alternatives like sparklers or laser shows.

IDiwali is also a time for gifting. People exchange sweets, dry fruits, and small tokens of love. Many also buy gold or silver items as part of the tradition.


Post-Diwali Rituals

The day after Diwali marks Govardhan Puja and Bhai Dooj.

This is a day dedicated to Lord Krishna and is celebrated by preparing special meals and performing prayers.

On this day, brothers visit their sisters, and they both pray for each other's well-being. Sisters apply tilak (a ceremonial mark) on their brothers' foreheads and in return, brothers give gifts as a sign of their love and protection.


Best Ways to Celebrate Diwali

To make your Diwali celebrations even more memorable, here are some additional tips:

Use biodegradable diyas instead of plastic ones, Switch to LED lights to save energy, Opt for minimal or eco-friendly crackers, or avoid them altogether to reduce pollution.

OIf you live far from loved ones, you can celebrate Diwali virtually by arranging video calls, playing online games, or sending gifts and sweets via e-commerce platforms.

Diwali is a festival of sharing and spreading joy. Donate clothes, food, or money to those in need. Many families visit orphanages or old age homes to share sweets and happiness with others.

Diwali is about inner light and spiritual renewal. Consider including meditation or a gratitude ritual in your celebration to focus on the deeper meanings of the festival.
